Module 2 Summary

Wrap Up

In this module we studied floating point representation — how computers store and manipulate numbers that have fractional parts. We began with fractional binary numbers and built up to the full IEEE 754 encoding scheme, examining how sign, exponent, and fraction fields work together to represent an enormous range of values. We explored rounding modes and the arithmetic pitfalls that arise from finite precision. Finally, we looked at the posit number system as a modern alternative to IEEE 754.

Key Takeaways

  • Fractional binary numbers use a radix point, with bit positions to the right representing negative powers of 2 (1/2, 1/4, 1/8, etc.).
  • IEEE 754 is the dominant standard for floating point. A value is encoded using three fields: a sign bit, a biased exponent, and a fractional mantissa with an implicit leading 1 (for normalized values).
  • Special values in IEEE 754 include positive/negative infinity (exponent all 1s, fraction all 0s) and NaN (exponent all 1s, fraction nonzero). Denormalized numbers (exponent all 0s) allow representation of values very close to zero.
  • Rounding is unavoidable because most real numbers cannot be represented exactly. The default mode, round-to-even, minimizes statistical bias over many operations.
  • Precision loss is a fundamental limitation: floating point addition is not associative, and small numbers added to large numbers may be lost entirely. Overflow and underflow are also concerns.
  • Posits (Posit<n, es>) offer an alternative to IEEE 754 with variable-length regime bits, tapered precision (more precision near 1.0, less at extremes), no NaN, no wasted bit patterns, and a single special value (NaR) for undefined results.
